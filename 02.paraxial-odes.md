## Introduction

In electron optics, the trajectory of an electron moving predominantly along the optic axis $z$ can be described using the paraxial approximation, in which transverse displacements $r(z)$ are small and ray angles satisfy $|r'(z)| \ll 1$.
Under this assumption we retain only terms up to first order in r (equivalently up to quadratic order in the potential expansion), and neglect higher-order transverse terms that give rise to aberrations.

### Electrostatic Focusing

We have already seen that in a cylindrically symmetric, charge-free region, the electrostatic potential satisfies Laplace's equation. Near the axis, we can retain the quadratic term only:

$$
\Phi(r,z)
=
\Phi(z)
-\frac{r^2}{4}\Phi''(z)
+\mathcal{O}(r^4).
$$

The transverse electric field then follows from $\mathbf{E}=-\nabla \Phi$,

$$
E_r
=
\frac{r}{2}\Phi''(z).
$$

```mathematica
paraxialPotential[r_, z_] = \[Phi][z] - r^2/4 \[Phi]''[z];
paraxialRadialField[r_, z_] = -Derivative[1, 0][paraxialPotential][r, z]
```

The Lorentz force for a purely electrostatic system is

$$
m \frac{d^2 \mathbf{r}}{dt^2} = -e \mathbf{E}.
$$

Using $z$ as the independent variable and noting that the axial velocity satisfies

$$
v_z^2 = \frac{2e}{m}\Phi(z),
$$

we transform derivatives via

$$
\frac{d}{dt} = v_z \frac{d}{dz}.
$$

Keeping only paraxial terms (linear in $r$ and $r'$), the transverse equation becomes

$$
r''(z)
+
\frac{\Phi'(z)}{2\Phi(z)} r'(z)
+
\frac{\Phi''(z)}{4\Phi(z)} r(z)
= 0.
$$

```mathematica
squaredVelocity[z_] := 2 \[Phi][z]/m;
chainRuleDoubleTimeDerivative = 
  squaredVelocity[z] r''[z] + D[squaredVelocity[z], z]/2 r'[z];
Simplify[m chainRuleDoubleTimeDerivative + paraxialRadialField[r, z]]
Expand[%/ (2 \[Phi][z])]
```

> The coefficient $\Phi''(z)/(4\Phi(z))$ arises directly from the axial curvature of the harmonic potential, while the term $\Phi'(z)/(2\Phi(z))$ reflects longitudinal acceleration as the electron gains kinetic energy.

### Magnetic Focusing

For a purely magnetic, axially symmetric lens, the near-axis magnetic field satisfies
$$
B_r = -\frac{r}{2} \frac{dB_z}{dz}.
$$

The Lorentz force is now given by:
$$
m \frac{d\mathbf{v}}{dt}= -e\, \mathbf{v} \times \mathbf{B}.
$$

In the paraxial limit, the dominant effect is Larmor rotation about the axis.
Eliminating the azimuthal motion yields an effective radial restoring force proportional to $B_z^2$.

$$
r''(z)
+
\frac{\eta^2 B^2(z)}{4\Phi_0}
\, r(z)
= 0,
$$

where

$$
\eta = \sqrt{\frac{|e|}{2m}}
$$

and $\Phi_0$ is the accelerating potential (constant energy).

> Magnetic lenses focus not through axial curvature, but through rotational dynamics: the conserved canonical angular momentum produces a quadratic restoring force proportional to $B^2$.

### General paraxial ray equation

For an axially symmetric system with electrostatic potential $\Phi(z)$ and magnetic field $B(z)$, the transverse motion obeys

$$
r''(z)
+
\frac{\Phi'(z)}{2\Phi(z)} r'(z)
+
\left(
\frac{\Phi''(z)}{4\Phi(z)}
+
\frac{\eta^2 B^2(z)}{4\Phi_0}
\right)
r(z)
=0.
$$

This second-order ODE forms the foundation of paraxial electron optics. Its solutions describe principal and marginal rays, while its coefficients encode the focusing properties of electrostatic curvature and magnetic rotation.

```mathematica
linearizedODE[\[Phi]0_][\[Phi]_] := Block[{r, z},
  r''[z] + \[Phi]'[z]/(2 \[Phi][z]) r'[z] + \[Phi]''[z]/(4 \[Phi][z])
      r[z] == 0
  ]
linearizedODE[\[Phi]0_, \[Eta]_][B_] := Block[{r, z},
  r''[z] + (\[Eta]^2 B[z]^2)/(4 \[Phi]0) r[z] == 0
  ]
linearizedODE[\[Phi]0_, \[Eta]_][\[Phi]_, B_] := Block[{r, z},
  r''[z] + \[Phi]'[z]/(2 \[Phi][z])
      r'[z] + (\[Phi]''[z]/(4 \[Phi][z]) + (\[Eta]^2 B[z]^2)/(
       4 \[Phi]0)) r[z] == 0
  ]

physicalConstants = <|
   "e" -> QuantityMagnitude[Quantity["ElementaryCharge"], "SIBase"], 
   "m" -> QuantityMagnitude[Quantity["ElectronMass"], "SIBase"], 
   "c" -> QuantityMagnitude[Quantity["SpeedOfLight"], "SIBase"],
   "\[Eta]" :> Sqrt[Abs[physicalConstants["e"]]/(2 physicalConstants["m"])]
   |>;
```

## Analytical Axial Fields

To build intuition and enable reproducible calculations, we begin by working with analytical expressions for the on-axis fields of idealized electron lenses. 
These models capture the essential physics of focusing while remaining simple enough to manipulate symbolically and solve numerically.

### Electrostatic Round Lens (Schiske Field)

For electrostatic focusing, we use the Schiske potential, an analytical model for the on-axis electrostatic potential of an Einzel-type lens:

$$
\phi_{\mathrm{Schiske}}(z)
= \phi_0
\left(
1 - \frac{k^2}{1 + \left(z/a\right)^2}
\right)
$$

where $\Phi_0$ is the accelerating potential far from the lens, $k$ controls the strength of the electrostatic focusing, and a sets the axial extent of the lens region.

Physically, this potential corresponds to an Einzel lens: a three-electrode electrostatic lens in which the entrance and exit electrodes are held at the same potential, while the central electrode is biased. 
Electrons are decelerated and re-accelerated as they traverse the lens, producing focusing without a net change in beam energy.

```mathematica
electrostaticField["schiske"][a_, k_, \[Phi]0_][z_] = \[Phi]0 (1 - k^2/(1 + (z/a)^2));
```

### Magnetic Round Lens (Glaser Field)

A widely used model for the axial magnetic field of a rotationally symmetric magnetic lens is the Glaser bell-shaped field, given by
$$
B_{\mathrm{Glaser}}(z)
= \frac{B_ 0}{1 + \left(z/a\right)^2}
$$
where $B_0$ is the peak axial magnetic field strength, and a sets the effective half-width of the lens and controls how rapidly the field falls off along the optic axis.

This field approximates the axial field produced by a solenoidal magnetic lens with finite pole-piece separation.
While it does not correspond to an exact coil geometry, it closely reproduces the focusing properties of practical magnetic round lenses and is widely used in analytical aberration theory.

```mathematica
magneticField["glaser"][a_, B0_][z_] = B0/(1 + (z/a)^2);
```

## Paraxial Ray Solutions

The paraxial ray equation derived in the previous section is a linear, second-order ordinary differential equation in the transverse coordinate $r(z)$.
As a consequence, its solution space is two-dimensional: any paraxial ray trajectory can be written as a linear combination of two linearly independent solutions.

It is therefore natural to choose a convenient basis of solutions with a clear physical interpretation.
In electron optics, these are known as the primary ray and the marginal ray.

### Primary Ray

The primary ray describes the trajectory of a ray that starts with a finite transverse displacement but zero initial slope:
$$
g(z_ 0) = 1, \quad g'(z_ 0) = 0
$$

Physically, this corresponds to a ray that originates off-axis but initially travels parallel to the optic axis. 
The primary ray encodes how transverse positions in the object plane are imaged by the lens.

### Marginal Ray

The marginal ray describes the trajectory of a ray that starts on-axis but with a finite initial angle:
$$
h(z_ 0) = 0, \quad h'(z_ 0) = 1
$$
This ray represents the extreme ray in an angular pencil and encodes how angles at the object plane are transformed by the lens.

### General Solutions
Because the paraxial equation is linear, any paraxial ray with initial conditions
$$
r(z_ 0) = r_ 0, \quad r'(z_ 0) = r_0'
$$
can be written as a linear combination of the primary and marginal rays:
$$
r(z) = r_0\, g(z) + r_ 0'\, h(z)
$$

This decomposition makes the role of each ray explicit: $g(z)$ propagates transverse position; $h(z)$ propagates angular deviation.

### Linear Imaging Properties

Once the primary and marginal rays are known, the fundamental linear imaging properties of the lens follow directly.

#### Focal Plane

The focal plane is defined as the axial position where a ray entering parallel to the optic axis is brought to a focus. 
This corresponds to the zero crossing of the primary ray: $g(z_f)=0$.
The focal length is then measured relative to the chosen reference plane.

#### Image Plane
The image plane is defined as the axial position where a point source on the optic axis is imaged to a point.
This corresponds to the zero crossing of the marginal ray: $h(z_i)=0$. 
At this plane, rays emerging from a single object point intersect again.

#### Magnification

The transverse magnification is given by the value of the primary ray evaluated at the image plane: $M=g(z_i)$. 
This follows directly from the general solution: a point at transverse position $r_0$ in the object plane is mapped to $r(z_i)=r_0 g(z_i)$ in the image plane.

::::{tip} Wolfram language source code
:class: dropdown
:open: false

```mathematica

paraxialSolution["schiske"] = With[{
    a = 0.025,
    \[Phi]0 = 5,
    k = 1/Sqrt[2],
    startPosition = -0.5,
    endPosition = 1.0
    },
   ParametricNDSolveValue[
    {
     
     linearizedODE[\[Phi]0][
      electrostaticField["schiske"][a, k, \[Phi]0]],
     r[startPosition] == initialTransverseDisplacement,
     r'[startPosition] == initialSlope,
     
     WhenEvent[r[z] == 0 && z > 0, Sow[z]]
     
     },
    r, {z, startPosition, endPosition},
    {initialTransverseDisplacement, initialSlope},
    Method -> {"ParametricCaching" -> None}
    ]
   ];

{primaryRay["schiske"], {{focalPlane["schiske"]}}} = 
  Reap[paraxialSolution["schiske"][1, 0]];
{marginalRay["schiske"], {{imagePlane["schiske"]}}} = 
  Reap[paraxialSolution["schiske"][0, 1]];
magnification["schiske"] = 
primaryRay["schiske"][imagePlane["schiske"]]
```
