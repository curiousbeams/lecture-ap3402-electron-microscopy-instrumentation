## Laplace Expansion Exercises

::::{exercise}
:label: ex_order-6-expansion

In @analytical-expansion, we derived the two leading non-trivial terms in the near-axis expansion.

:::{embed} eq_near_axis_expansion
:::

- Do the same for the next few terms and compare the magnitude of the $r^4$ and $r^6$ terms to the leading $r^2$ term for a typical lens profile.
- Under what physical conditions is truncation at order $r^2$ justified?

::::

::::{solution} ex_order-6-expansion
:class: dropdown

**Higher-order Laplace expansion**

We extend the near-axis expansion by determining the higher-order radial derivatives of the potential using Laplace’s equation.
As before, we enforce that the Laplacian of the truncated series vanishes order-by-order in $r$.

The coefficients can be obtained recursively by solving the Laplace equation for each power of $r$, and expressing higher-order radial derivatives in terms of axial derivatives:

```mathematica
evenExpansion = 
 Normal[Series[(\[Phi][r, z] + \[Phi][-r, z])/2, {r, 0, 6}]]
 evenLaplacian = 
 Simplify[Laplacian[evenExpansion, {r, \[Theta], z}, "Cylindrical"]]

 order0Substitutions = Solve[
   Coefficient[evenLaplacian, r, 0] == 0,
   Derivative[2, 0][\[Phi]][0, z]
   ] // First

order2Substitutions = Solve[
    Coefficient[evenLaplacian, r, 2] == 0,
    Derivative[4, 0][\[Phi]][0, z]
    ] // First;
order2Substitutions = 
 order2Substitutions /. D[order0Substitutions, {z, 2}]

order4Substitutions = Solve[
    Coefficient[evenLaplacian, r, 4] == 0,
    Derivative[6, 0][\[Phi]][0, z]
    ] // First;

order4Substitutions = 
 order4Substitutions /. D[order2Substitutions, {z, 2}]

```

This yields the recursive relations:

$$
\begin{align*}
\Phi^{(2,0)}(z) &\rightarrow -\frac{1}{2} \Phi^{(0,2)}(z) \\
\Phi^{(4,0)}(z) &\rightarrow \frac{3}{8} \Phi^{(0,4)}(z) \\
\Phi^{(6,0)}(z) &\rightarrow -\frac{5}{16} \Phi^{(0,6)}(z)
\end{align*}
$$

Substituting these into the even expansion gives the near-axis harmonic expansion:

```mathematica
harmonicExpansion = 
 evenExpansion /. 
  Join[order0Substitutions, order2Substitutions, order4Substitutions]
```

$$
\Phi(r,z) = \Phi_0(z) - \frac{1}{4} r^2\, \Phi^{(0,2)}(z) + \frac{1}{64} r^4\, \Phi^{(0,4)}(z) -\frac{1}{2304} r^6\, \Phi^{(0,6)}(z) + \mathcal{O}(r^8)
$$

**Paraxial truncation justification**

Keeping only the $r^2$ term corresponds to the paraxial approximation.
This is justified when:
  - rays remain close to the optical axis ($r<<L$, for a characteristic axial length $L$ over which the potential varies)
  - ray angles are small ($dr / dz \ll 1$)

Under these conditions the field is well approximated by its lowest-order radial dependence, and higher-order terms contribute only small corrections.
Physically, the neglected $r^4$ and $r^6$ terms are responsible for geometric aberrations such as spherical aberration.
As rays move farther from the axis, these higher-order terms grow and the paraxial approximation breaks down.

## Paraxial ODE Exercises

:::{exercise}
:label: ex_glaser

For the @glaser-field:
- Show that narrowing the field increases peak curvature.
- Predict qualitatively how focal length scales with peak field and strength.
- Verify these predictions numerically.

:::

::::{solution} ex_glaser
:class: dropdown

**Glaser field curvature**

The curvature of the @glaser-field at the lens center is given by:

```mathematica
magneticField["glaser"][a_, B0_][z_] = B0/(1 + (z/a)^2);
magneticField["glaser"][a, B0]''[0]
```

$$
B''(0) = -\frac{2 B_0}{a^2}.
$$

Reducing $a$, narrows the field, and therefore increases the peak curvature.

**Focal length scaling**

Recall that for a magnetic field, the paraxial ray equation is:

:::{embed} eq_paraxial_magnetic
:::


In the thin-lens approximation the focusing strength is proportional to the integrated field squared

```mathematica
Integrate[
 magneticField["glaser"][a, B0][z]^2, {z, -\[Infinity], \[Infinity]}, 
 Assumptions -> a > 0
 ]
```

$$
\frac{1}{f}
\propto
\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} B^2(z)\,dz
=
\frac{\pi}{2} a B_0^2.
$$

Thus:
- Increasing the peak field $B_0$ strengthens focusing quadratically.
- Increasing the width *a* strengthens focusing linearly.

:::{exercise} coding required
:label: ex_4f_system

For the @glaser-field or @schiske-field:
- Design a two-lens system by adding two axial fields separated by a distance $d$ and solving the paraxial ODE numerically.
- Find the distance $d$ to produce unit magnification.
- Comment how, for electron diffraction, this setup relates to [](wiki:Friedel's_law).

:::

::::{solution} ex_4f_system
:class: dropdown

**Glaser "4f" system**

We can combine axial lens fields by summing them.
E.g. for two separated @glaser-field:

$$
B(z) = B_{\mathrm{Glaser}}(z) + B_{\mathrm{Glaser}}(z-s),
$$

and solving the paraxial ray equation numerically.

```mathematica
magneticField["glaser-4f"][a_, B0_, s_][z_] = 
  magneticField["glaser"][a, B0][z] + 
   magneticField["glaser"][a, B0][z - s];
```

Using the focal length extracted for a single Glaser lens, a natural first guess for unit magnification is the 4f configuration: $s \approx 2f$.

:::{figure} ./figures/glaser_rays_4f.svg
:label: glaser_rays_4f_fig
Glaser "4f" configuration, illustrating unit magnification when the two fields are spaced $\approx 1.95f$ apart.
:::

Numerically optimizing the lens separation shows that the correct value is slightly smaller ($s \approx 1.95f$ for the parameters used here).
The outgoing rays are then parallel and the image height equals the object height, corresponding to unit magnification.
The small deviation from $2f$ arises because the Glaser field violated the thin lens approximation, whith focusing action occuring over a finite axial extent of the magnetic field.

**Relation to electron diffraction**

In a symmetric $4f$ system, the plane halfway between the lenses is the Fourier plane of the first lens.
In electron diffraction experiments this is where the diffraction pattern forms.

Because diffraction from a real potential obeys

$$
I(\mathbf{k}) = I(-\mathbf{k}),
$$

the diffraction pattern is centrosymmetric (Friedel's law).

## Transfer Matrices Exercises

:::{exercise}
:label: ex_transfer_matrix

For _imaging_, the image height depends solely on the object height, not angle.
Given a transfer matrix:
$$
M = \begin{pmatrix} A & B \\ C & D \end{pmatrix}
$$

- Show that this requires $B=0$ and that magnification is given by $A$.
- Reconcile this observation with the following (incorrect) statement:
  > For a thin-lens, where the transfer matrix is given by $M = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ -1/f & 1 \end{pmatrix}$, this would seem to imply magnification is independent of the focal length, $f$.
- Extend your reasoning to derive the thin-lens formula: $\frac{1}{f} = \frac{1}{d_{\mathrm{obj}}} + \frac{1}{d_{\mathrm{img}}}$.

:::

:::{solution} ex_transfer_matrix
:class: dropdown

**Imaging condition**

For a ray vector $\begin{pmatrix} r \\r' \end{pmatrix}$, the transfer matrix acts as

$$
\begin{pmatrix} r_2 \\r_2' \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} A & B \\ C & D  \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} r_1 \\r_1' \end{pmatrix}.
$$

For imaging, the image height must depend only on the object height and not on the launch angle.
Therefore the coefficient multiplying $r_1'$ must vanish:

$$
r_2 = A r_1 + B r_1'.
$$

This requires $B=0$, giving

$$
r_2 = A r_1,
$$

so the magnification is $m = A$.

**Trick question**

The statement about the thin-lens matrix is misleading because it applies only to the lens element itself, not to the full imaging system.
A real imaging system also includes propagation from the object to the lens and from the lens to the image:

$$
M = \underbrace{\begin{pmatrix} 1 & d_{\mathrm{img}} \\ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}}_{\text{propagation to image}} \underbrace{\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ -1/f & 1 \end{pmatrix}}_{\text{thin lens}} \underbrace{\begin{pmatrix} 1 & d_{\mathrm{obj}} \\ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}}_{\text{propagation from object}}.
$$

Expanding this out we obtain:

```mathematica
systemTransferMatrix = 
 transferMatrix["propagation"][dImg] . transferMatrix["lens"][f] . 
  transferMatrix["propagation"][dObj]
```

$$
M = \begin{pmatrix} 1-d_{\mathrm{img}}/f & d_{\mathrm{obj}} + d_{\mathrm{img}} (1-d_{\mathrm{obj}}/f) \\ -1/f & 1-d_{\mathrm{obj}}/f  \end{pmatrix}.
$$

**Thin lens formula**

The imaging condition requires the full system to satisfy $B=0$.
Setting the upper-right element to zero, and solving for $1/f$ we recover the thin-lens formula:

```mathematica
solveForF = SolveValues[systemTransferMatrix[[1, 2]] == 0, f] // First;
Expand[1/solveForF]
```
$$
\frac{1}{f} = \frac{1}{d_{\mathrm{obj}}} + \frac{1}{d_{\mathrm{img}}}
$$

Thus the familiar thin-lens formula follows directly from the transfer-matrix imaging condition.

## Non-Paraxial ODE Exercises

:::{exercise}
:label: ex_constant_larmor

- Compute the total Larmor rotation angle given by @eq_larmor_rotation_angle, for a lens with a constant magnetic field $B$, with length $L$.
- Discuss why magnetic focusing survives for a constant field lens.

:::

:::{solution} ex_constant_larmor
:class: dropdown

**Uniform Larmor rotation**

For a constant magnetic field $B$ over a length $L$, the Larmor frequency

$$
\Omega = \frac{\eta B}{2\sqrt{\Phi_0}}
$$

is constant, giving the total Larmor rotation

$$
\theta_L
=
\int_0^L \Omega\,dz
=
\frac{\eta B L}{2\sqrt{\Phi_0}}.
$$

The beam undergoes a uniform Larmor rotation proportional to the integrated magnetic field.

**Constant field focusing**

Even when the axial field is constant, magnetic focusing does not vanish.  
From Maxwell’s equations, the near-axis radial field is

$$
B_r = -\frac{r}{2}\frac{dB_z}{dz}.
$$

While $B_z$ is constant in the interior of the lens, it must change at the entrance and exit of the field region.
These fringe regions therefore produce non-zero $B_r$, which generates the initial radial Lorentz force responsible for focusing.

Thus a “constant-field lens" focuses primarily through the interaction of the fringe fields with the uniform Larmor rotation.
:::

:::{exercise} coding required
:label: ex_ray_trajectories

Using 3D @schiske-non-paraxial ray tracing for increasing launch angles:
- Plot the ray-traced positions at the image plane [^1] for `order=1` and `order=3`.
- Quantify the deviation from the paraxial prediction as launch angle is increased, and comment how this relates to spherical aberration.
:::

::::{solution} ex_ray_trajectories
:class: dropdown

Admittedly this exercise is somewhat tricky.  
Its main goal is to build familiarity with inspecting and plotting ray-traced trajectories, which will be useful later when we perform aberration fitting.

:::{figure} ./figures/ray_tracing_trajectories_figure.svg
:label: ray_tracing_trajectories_fig
Ray-tracing trajectories for a Schiske field expanded to different orders.
:::

A key subtlety is which plane we choose to evaluate the ray positions.

If the trajectories are evaluated at the paraxial (Gaussian) image plane, the ray endpoints form increasingly large circles as the launch angle increases.
This occurs for all expansion orders and reflects the expected deviation from paraxial imaging.
However, part of this deviation arises because the non-paraxial rays are evaluated at a plane that is **slightly out of focus.

If instead we determine the plane that minimizes the ray envelope numerically, a different trend appears: for large launch angles, increasing the expansion order reduces the residual spread of ray endpoints.
This indicates that higher-order expansions better capture the non-paraxial dynamics.

The remaining angle-dependent spread of the rays corresponds to spherical aberration, where rays launched at larger angles focus at different axial positions.

::::

[^1]: The image plane here can be taken either as the paraxial (Gaussian) image plane, or the plane which minimizes the ray envelope numerically.
