::::{solution} ex_order-6-expansion
:class: dropdown

**Higher-Order Laplace Derivation**

We extend the near-axis expansion by determining the higher-order radial derivatives of the potential using Laplace’s equation.
As before, we enforce that the Laplacian of the truncated series vanishes order-by-order in $r$.

The coefficients can be obtained recursively by solving the Laplace equation for each power of $r$, and expressing higher-order radial derivatives in terms of axial derivatives:

```mathematica
evenExpansion = 
 Normal[Series[(\[Phi][r, z] + \[Phi][-r, z])/2, {r, 0, 6}]]
 evenLaplacian = 
 Simplify[Laplacian[evenExpansion, {r, \[Theta], z}, "Cylindrical"]]

 order0Substitutions = Solve[
   Coefficient[evenLaplacian, r, 0] == 0,
   Derivative[2, 0][\[Phi]][0, z]
   ] // First

order2Substitutions = Solve[
    Coefficient[evenLaplacian, r, 2] == 0,
    Derivative[4, 0][\[Phi]][0, z]
    ] // First;
order2Substitutions = 
 order2Substitutions /. D[order0Substitutions, {z, 2}]

order4Substitutions = Solve[
    Coefficient[evenLaplacian, r, 4] == 0,
    Derivative[6, 0][\[Phi]][0, z]
    ] // First;

order4Substitutions = 
 order4Substitutions /. D[order2Substitutions, {z, 2}]

```

This yields the recursive relations:

$$
\begin{align*}
\Phi^{(2,0)}(z) &\rightarrow -\frac{1}{2} \Phi^{(0,2)}(z) \\
\Phi^{(4,0)}(z) &\rightarrow \frac{3}{8} \Phi^{(0,4)}(z) \\
\Phi^{(6,0)}(z) &\rightarrow -\frac{5}{16} \Phi^{(0,6)}(z)
\end{align*}
$$

Substituting these into the even expansion gives the near-axis harmonic expansion:

```mathematica
harmonicExpansion = 
 evenExpansion /. 
  Join[order0Substitutions, order2Substitutions, order4Substitutions]
```

$$
\Phi(r,z) = \Phi_0(z) - \frac{1}{4} r^2\, \Phi^{(0,2)}(z) + \frac{1}{64} r^4\, \Phi^{(0,4)}(z) -\frac{1}{2304} r^6\, \Phi^{(0,6)}(z) + \mathcal{O}(r^8)
$$

**Paraxial Truncation Justification**

Keeping only the $r^2$ term corresponds to the paraxial approximation.
This is justified when:
  - rays remain close to the optical axis ($r<<L$, for a characteristic axial length $L$ over which the potential varies)
  - ray angles are small ($dr / dz \ll 1$)

Under these conditions the field is well approximated by its lowest-order radial dependence, and higher-order terms contribute only small corrections.
Physically, the neglected $r^4$ and $r^6$ terms are responsible for geometric aberrations such as spherical aberration.
As rays move farther from the axis, these higher-order terms grow and the paraxial approximation breaks down.

Numerical finite element solution and analytical axial expansion slices with increasing distance from the optical axis.

The numerical and analytical solutions agree well close to the optical axis, but begin to diverge at approximately $r \gtrsim 0.002 $, as we approach the electrode boundary.
Regions far from the electrodes remain reasonably accurate even for much larger radii (e.g. $r \approx 0.01$), reflecting the fact that the field varies more slowly there.

Including higher-order terms ($r^4$, $r^6$) does not significantly extend the radius of validity.
This indicates that the dominant limitation is not truncation order, but the fact that the Taylor expansion itself ceases to converge once the radius approaches the electrodes.
